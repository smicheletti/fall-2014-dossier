Workshop 1: IBM

Designers: Claire Kohler & Erin Heuber

September 10th, 2014

With Rio Sauer, Daniel Shipley, Cody Alder, Briana Zimbleman, and Edward Rendon

Our workshop with IBM focused primarily on design research processes along with some rapid prototyping. In class we began by identifying a problem and creating a stakeholders map. From there, we explored "Needs a way to _____ so that _____" statements as well as a "Says, Thinks, Does, Feels" map in order to better identify the needs of our persona. This was followed by various forms of observational research. Edward and I participated in fly-on-the-wall research in the evening watching runners while others in our group performed interviews and other observational research methods. We then rearranged our post-it maps due to new information while considering ways we could better execute research in the future. Our end product, "North Star," focuses on the specific needs runners experience when out at night or the early morning. The neon bands help ensure visibility to make the user feel safer while also incorporating in moisture wicking and cooling technology in the fabric.

Workshop 2: Sol Design

Designer: Beth Ferguson

October 1st, 2014

With Savannah Hicks and Rio Sauer

For this workshop my teammates and I decided to focus on the re-design of the two current solar charging stations on campus. We focused on issues that the current solar stations struggle with such as: confusion with UFCU due to coloring, no apparent signage, temporary information in the form of magnets, and not enough information. Along with alterations for aesthetic appeal, we proposed to implement features to the station such as touch-charging power as well as informational kiosks at the center of the station. The branding and style reflects that of a Jetsons-era and harkens back to imaginative sci-fi where even personal jetpacks are possible. An app will accompany the station to give students information while also gaining data on their usage of the station. With future technology, stickers would allow users to wirelessly charge devices since we found many students don't bring phone chargers to campus on a daily basis. The station would be placed at another prime location on campus right outside the Union. This area gets a good deal of traffic as well as ample sunlight throughout the day.

Workshop 3: GSD&M

Designer: Will Chau

November 5th, 2014

Charged with using the "1+1=3" method to create something more than merely an advertisement for a Ford-F150, my final proposal includes a special edition Parks and Rec truck. Based on an early idea of Ford "carrying the world," I worked to find a way to encourage Ford to in some way be more eco-friendly. While a re-design of the truck's mechanics was out of the question, the company can still help the environment through this special edition truck. The added on cost would go toward implementing the special truck-bed features as well as a portion being donated to the buyer's local Parks and Rec department. The truck-bed would have rearrangeable compartment walls as well as a chest containing camping and survival essentials. Along with this special edition truck, Ford would release a series of advertisements owning up to producing gas-guzzler trucks while also emphasizing that they realize this and wish to help out the environment in other ways. The Parks and Rec edition would serve its goal of being a tough, strong truck while also contributing in some other way to the preservation of our planet.

Workshop 4: Critical Design

Designer: Becky Nasadowski

October 29th, 2014

With Savannah Hicks & Rio Sauer

Critical design can take many forms and methods of communication; however, Savannah, Rio, and I decided early on that we wished to explore critical design through satire. "Bad Block" helps deflect any racism, sexism, prejudice, or bullying through literally blocking words or sights by a blindfold and headset while the cheek-turner encourages the user to be a better person by accepting what is physically done to them by turning the other cheek. What should be obvious to the viewer (or user) is that this simple, universal solution to a huge societal problem is utterly ineffective. As designers, we must hone in on specific ways to solve problems, even if that means the end result may not be perfect for every person. Through its ridiculous proposal, Bad Block serves to provoke thought in the viewer of the large issues as well as current proposed solutions that often miss the mark.

Workshop 5: Industry

Designers: Bobby Dixon, Bart Kibbe, Brian Maclaskey

November 19th, 2014

With Caroline Wallace, Meredith Gunn, and Savannah Hicks

Posed with the phrase "Watch your step" we decided to raise students' situational awareness on campus through a holiday-themed campaign. "Be Present" consisted of a vinyl "watch your step" adhered to a stair step going to the art building covered by wrapping paper with "be present" screen printed on it wrapped up nicely in golden ribbon. Our hope was that as students walked on the wrapping paper, unaware of their surroundings due to their phone or other stimuli, and ultimately tear it up to reveal the "watch your step." While not everyone seemed to notice it as they walked over, surprisingly many people skipped over the step and were more aware than we expected.

Workshop 6: Wearable Tech

Designer: Helene Day Fraser

December 3rd, 2014

With Savannah Hicks & Rio Sauer

Starting off with a pair of embroidered jeans from Goodwill, we created a narrative of a middle-aged white woman named Mary Jo-Jo interested in gardening. After our narrative we were encouraged to ideate on ways in which we could use fashion as a narrative and conversation starter. One of the ideas we iterated on at Helene's request was "centipede pants." These pants contain multiple legs and encourage users to work together in order to move as a way to bond and become closer. However, we changed course and instead focused on The Tracksuit which integrated technology and clothing together.
